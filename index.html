About Me

When I left the UK to study in Canada, I knew I wanted to maintain an interdisciplinary approach to my studies. As such, I spent my four years in Canada cultivating an understanding of both Philosophy and Biology. Only after a few years of this did it become clear to me that the two fields were not disjointed and distinct; instead, they were attempting to provide an understanding of ourselves and our place in the world around us and give prescriptions about what we should do given this knowledge. It is this point where the two meet that I find most interesting. In my studies I have cultivated skills in concise and clear scientific writing, developing original theses, coding in HTML and R (self-taught), First Order and Mathematical Logic, and have been exposed to an array of different literature (both scientific, philosophical and otherwise). My interests outside of my studies include film photography, reading and writing, hiking, skiing, an interest in wildlife that took me to Costa Rica for 2 weeks following my graduation, cultivating a variety of houseplants (I have successfully grown tomatoes and avocados indoors), and cooking.

An Evalutation of the Modularity of Leg Striping in Horses

In my independent studies with Professor Abouheif at McGill, we were assessing the possibility of leg striping as an atavism or ancestral potential. It became clear that, in order to complete this project, it is necessary to understand the way striping emerges and whether there are regional biases across and within legs. To assess this, we counted the number of stripes and assessed their location using ImageJ. With this coordinate data, I constructed heat maps to demonstrate the pattern. The paper is incomplete but the other undergraduate researcher and I are working to finish and publish it when possible.

in Philosophy

These three Philosophy papers span across my last two years and were all Final Essays for 4th year courses. First, PHIL 480 was a class on phenomenology and technology using the works of Martin Heidegger and Edmund Husserl. My paper focused on the Natural Sciences as providing a means of escape from the issues of a technologically enframed perspective, and their ultimate failure to overcome these issues due to a fundamental technisation of their very focus (ie Nature). The second, PHIL 474 was an introduction to Heidegger and Phenomenology through a detailed analysis of his Being and Time. My essay focused on the phenomenological versus psychology perspectives of Anxiety and my belief that the Phenomenological perspectives ultimately provides answers and a view on Anxiety that overcomes the issues of psychology. This essay also drew from Husserl's Crisis in the Sciences, and critiqued the limitations around the Scientific perspective. The final class was taken in my last year - PHIL 475. This class was on the limits of Phenomenology and the responses to such limits. In order to frame the limits, I took a Derridian Deconstructive position, and looked at the works of Kierkegaard, Freud, and Heidegger on Anxiety. This enabled a holistic and deeper view of the notion as well as possible prescriptions (a capacity that Heidegger on his own lacks). These essays all contributed to my receiving an A, A and A- in each of the respective classes and can be read below.
